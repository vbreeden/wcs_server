When dancing West Coast Swing, what makes some songs more danceable than others?

When dancing West Coast Swing, some songs seem to just work. You can feel the build-up of energy in the song, you can hear breaks approaching to the music, and you feel like you know how to shape your dance the through the entire experience. Other songs are not so giving. They are either confusing, too repetitive, have a rhythm that's hard to follow, or just don't feel right.
For all of those songs that just work, what features do they have in common? Is it their rhythm, does it have to do with their phrasing, or perhaps it's how the chord progressions are arranged? Is there something more subtle that these songs have in common? As far as I am aware this question hasn't been formally answered, and this site is intended was built to take the first step towards answering that question.

Why do we associate certain movements with specific sounds music?

Whether trained as dancers or not, melodies, rhythms, and time signatures inspire people of various backgrounds to move in similar ways. When people move in ways that don't match the music it looks off to an audience. To demonstrate the point, here's a link to Hayley Westenra's Dark Waltz. Start the song to playing and then open the 1st Place McMaster Hip-Hop Dance Competition. While Dark Waltz is playing watch the Hip Hop video with its audio silenced. The movement of the dancers is not in sync with music. Now compare this to dancers dancing a waltz to Dark Waltz. Now we start to see agreement between the music in the movement.
This begs the question though, what is it about smoother transitions between notes that inspires us to move more smoothly from one foot to the next, or more specifically, to have a smoother transfer of weight from one foot to the other through the entirely of the beat, and why is it that more rhythmic movement inspires a quicker transition of weight either at the beginning or end of a beat (closer to the 1 or "ah" when counting 1-e-&-ah)?
Moving forward with this idea, can we extract kinetic information from the music? If so, are is the movement-to-music relationship cultural, universal, or is it completely subjective and therefore entirely dependent on what the dancer is taught? I posit that at it's most restricted, it's cultural and that most people will respond similarly to any given musical piece (i.e. staccato music will result in staccato movement and legato music will result in legato movement, even in untrained dancers). If this is the case then we can develop algorithms to extract this kinetic information from the music.
